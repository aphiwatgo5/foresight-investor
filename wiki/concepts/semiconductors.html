Semiconductors

Definition: The chip value chain — designing and manufacturing the integrated circuits that
power all computing. A conviction-4, accelerating megatrend on the trend-radar, inseparable
from ai-infrastructure (the demand engine) and energy-transition (the power constraint).
This page treats the theme as four sub-segments: (a) foundry/manufacturing, (b) semicap
equipment, (c) logic/compute design, (d) memory (HBM focus).

Research/insight only — no buy/sell advice. Every figure is tagged (fact)/(est.)/(inference)
with period and source. "No reliable figure found" used where data is unverified.

1. Definition & scope

Semiconductors are the foundational layer of modern electronics. The investable theme spans the
whole chain: raw materials and wafers → semicap equipment that builds fabs → foundries
that manufacture chips → fabless designers of logic/compute → memory makers → packaging
and test → the AI-infrastructure buildout downstream. The 2024–2026 cycle is unusual: it is a
single-end-market boom (AI data-center compute) rather than a broad consumer/industrial
recovery — concentrating gains in leading-edge logic, HBM memory, and advanced packaging while
mature/trailing-edge and consumer-exposed segments lag.

3. Key structural drivers

Demand side
- AI accelerators. NVIDIA Data Center revenue $75.2B in Q1 FY2027 (quarter ended ~Apr-2026),
+92% YoY; data-center compute $60.4B, networking $14.8B (fact, NVIDIA 8-K). This single line item
pulls foundry, HBM, advanced packaging, and networking silicon.
- HBM supercycle. Memory is the tightest link. Micron sold out 2026 HBM capacity; SK Hynix and
Samsung scaling capacity ~50% in 2026; HBM4 mass production begins across all three in 2026
(web: Astute Group / DCD, 2026). HBM carries far higher margins than commodity DRAM.
- Custom silicon (ASIC). Hyperscaler in-house accelerators — Broadcom AI semi revenue guided
to ~$8.2B for Q1 FY2026, ~+100% YoY (web: Futurum, 2026); Marvell custom compute. Diversifies
demand beyond merchant GPUs.

Supply side
- Capacity concentration. TSMC ~70% of foundry revenue and the overwhelming majority of
leading-edge (≤5nm) capacity → single point of dependence (fact).
- Geographic concentration. Leading-edge logic concentrated in Taiwan; advanced lithography in
the Netherlands (ASML); HBM/DRAM in South Korea. Reshoring (US CHIPS-funded fabs, Intel Foundry,
TSMC Arizona) is slow and costly.
- Export controls. US restrictions on advanced chips/tools to China. Tokyo Electron expected
China sales to fall toward ~30% of mix in 2025 amid tightening curbs (web: TrendForce, 2025-06).
Controls protect Western leading-edge moats but cap China-exposed revenue and spur domestic
Chinese capacity buildout (long-run competitive risk).

  • Upstream → midstream: ASML's EUV is the gating tool for leading-edge; without it no <7nm.
    AMAT/LRCX/KLA/TEL supply deposition, etch, process-control, and (increasingly) advanced-packaging
    tooling — the latter critical for stacking HBM and chiplets.
  • Midstream → downstream: TSMC manufactures for NVIDIA, AMD, Broadcom, Apple, Qualcomm; its
    CoWoS advanced packaging is the bottleneck for AI accelerators. Memory makers feed HBM directly
    onto accelerator packages.
  • Cross-theme links: demand engine = ai-infrastructure; power constraint = energy-transition;
    networking silicon (Broadcom, Marvell, NVIDIA NVLink) bridges to the networking theme; gallium,
    germanium, rare earths, copper link to critical-minerals (export-control flashpoint).

5. Competitive landscape per sub-segment

  • (a) Foundry/manufacturing — high concentration, TSMC dominant. TSMC ~70% revenue share and
    near-monopoly at leading edge; gross margin guided 63–65% (Q1 2026) (web). Samsung Foundry
    a distant #2; Intel Foundry burning cash ($11.7B FY2025 operating loss, web: Intel 8-K /
    Tom's Hardware) while ramping 18A; GlobalFoundries focused on mature/specialty nodes. Moat:
    capital intensity + process leadership + CoWoS packaging.
  • (b) Semicap equipment — oligopoly with one monopoly. ASML = EUV lithography monopoly
    (100% of EUV) — the single most defensible position in the chain. AMAT and Lam lead
    deposition/etch; KLA dominates process-control/metrology (~50%+ margins). TEL strong in coat/
    develop/etch. Moat: IP, decades of process know-how, installed-base service revenue.
  • (c) Logic/compute design — NVIDIA dominant in merchant AI GPU. NVIDIA ~75% gross margin and
    the platform/CUDA moat; AMD the credible #2 (Instinct ramping); Broadcom/Marvell win on custom
    ASIC; Arm owns the CPU instruction-set royalty model; Qualcomm mobile-centric (memory-cost
    headwind). Moat: software ecosystem (NVIDIA), IP/royalty (Arm), design wins (Broadcom).
  • (d) Memory — oligopoly, HBM the prize. Three players. HBM share Q3 2025: SK Hynix ~53–62%,
    Samsung ~35%, Micron ~11%     (web: Astute/Silicon Analysts — sources vary on exact split). SK
    Hynix the HBM leader; Micron overtook Samsung in some 2025 reads; Samsung pushing HBM4 to
    recover. Moat: consolidated structure + HBM technical lead + capacity allocation.

7. Competitiveness read

Winning. The clearest winners sit where capital intensity and IP create durable
near-monopolies. ASML is the single best-positioned company in the chain — EUV is irreplaceable
and a €38.8B backlog locks in years of revenue (fact). TSMC wins on leading-edge + CoWoS,
compounding share to ~70% with 60%+ gross margins (fact). NVIDIA dominates merchant AI compute
on the CUDA moat with ~75% gross margin and DC revenue still +92% YoY (fact). In memory, SK Hynix
is the HBM winner — record 2025 with a 49% operating margin, out-earning Samsung (fact). KLA
quietly owns process-control at the sector's highest margins. These five capture the bulk of the
AI-cycle economics.

Losing / pressured. Intel is the clear laggard — $11.7B FY2025 foundry operating loss,
weakest revenue since 2010, betting the company on the 18A ramp (web). Samsung is losing the
HBM race it once led (overtaken by Micron in some 2025 reads), now playing catch-up via HBM4.
Qualcomm faces a memory-cost squeeze and limited AI-accelerator exposure. Among designers,
merchant-GPU challengers (AMD) are gaining but remain a distant #2, while custom-ASIC players
(Broadcom, Marvell) are structurally winning hyperscaler in-house silicon — a real medium-term
threat to merchant GPU share. Net: the chain's profits are concentrating in leading-edge logic,
EUV equipment, and HBM memory; mature-node, consumer-exposed, and sub-scale players are squeezed.

8. Risks / what kills the thesis

  • Cyclicality. Semis are historically boom-bust; the current up-cycle is driven by one end
    market (AI capex). If ai-infrastructure capex digests (see that page's circular-financing and
    capex-vs-revenue risks), the whole chain de-rates together (inference).
  • China / export controls. Tighter curbs cap China-exposed equipment revenue (TEL toward ~30%
    China mix, 2025) and accelerate Chinese domestic capacity → long-run share erosion at trailing
    edge; retaliation risk on critical minerals (gallium/germanium) (web/inference).
  • Capex digestion. SEMI sees equipment growing through 2027, but a hyperscaler capex pause would
    hit WFE first and hardest (inference).
  • Memory oversupply. HBM is tight now, but DRAM/NAND are commodity and prone to glut; SK Hynix +
    Samsung + Micron all expanding capacity ~50% in 2026 → 2027 oversupply risk if AI demand cools
    (inference). Memory is the most cyclical sub-segment.
  • Single-point-of-failure geography. Taiwan (TSMC) leading-edge concentration = geopolitical
    tail risk that would hit the entire downstream (inference).
