Space Economy

Definition: The commercialization of space — launch (Rocket Lab, SpaceX-private), satellite
connectivity / direct-to-cell (AST SpaceMobile), Earth-observation/data (Planet Labs), lunar/
in-space services (Intuitive Machines), and satellite comms (Globalstar). A long-dated,
high-variance frontier theme with binary, momentum-driven stocks. Conviction 2.5 on the
trend-radar — large eventual TAM, but early, speculative, and cash-burning at the edges.

(a) Launch — Rocket Lab (RKLB), SpaceX (private); (b) direct-to-cell / satellite connectivity

3. Key structural drivers

Demand: falling launch costs (reusability) opening new applications; satellite broadband +
direct-to-cell connectivity (closing coverage gaps); Earth-observation/geospatial data for
defense, climate, agriculture; lunar/government programs (NASA Artemis, defense). Supply:
reusable launch (SpaceX) collapsing cost-to-orbit; smallsat manufacturing scale; private capital +
SPAC-era listings funding the build-out (with the attendant dilution/burn). Defense budgets
(defense-security) are a key anchor customer.

4. Value-chain / linkage

Launch (RKLB, SpaceX put mass in orbit) → satellite manufacturers/operators (ASTS connectivity,
PL imaging, GSAT comms) → data/services (geospatial analytics, broadband, direct-to-cell) → end
users (defense, telecom, enterprise, government). Strong linkage to defense-security
(government/military demand + ISR), networking-optical (connectivity), and frontier-tech
sentiment. Capital-market access (macro-regime risk appetite) is existential for the
cash-burning names.

5. Competitive landscape

Early, fragmented, and execution-binary: SpaceX (private) dominates launch + Starlink, dwarfing
public peers; Rocket Lab is the leading public launch + space-systems play (the relative quality
name); ASTS (direct-to-cell) and LUNR (lunar) are pre-scale, milestone-dependent, dilutive; Planet
Labs has a real recurring-data business. Moats are mostly prospective — first-mover, spectrum
rights (ASTS/GSAT), constellation scale, launch cadence. Most public names are not yet
self-funding.

7. Competitiveness read

The space economy is a real, large, long-dated TAM (~$1T by 2030–2040) wrapped in early,
speculative, high-variance equities. The dominant asset — SpaceX — is private (the ~$2T IPO
figure is unverified rumor), so public investors are left with a thinner, riskier set. Among them,
Rocket Lab is the relative quality name: real revenue (+63.5% to $200.3M), a ~$2.2B backlog,
and Neutron-rocket optionality, though still unprofitable. Planet Labs has a genuine recurring
geospatial-data business (+26%). The edges — ASTS (direct-to-cell, missed Q1 by ~60%) and
LUNR (lunar, ultra-lumpy) — are binary, milestone-and-capital-dependent, and dilutive. These
are momentum stocks whose valuations swing on single launches/contracts and whose survival depends
on capital-market access. Net: a legitimate frontier worth a small, speculative sleeve (RKLB/PL
as the more investable core; ASTS/LUNR as lottery-ticket optionality) — but the early stage, cash
burn, and binary execution justify a 2.5, and the caps must be re-verified.

8. Risks / what kills the thesis

  • Execution/binary risk — single failed launches or missed milestones can halve a stock (ASTS, LUNR).
  • Cash burn + dilution — most public names aren't self-funding; capital-market access is existential.
  • Valuation/momentum — caps are sentiment-driven and volatile; data goes stale fast.
  • SpaceX dominance — the private leader could marginalize public peers in launch/broadband.
  • Regulatory/spectrum — licensing, orbital-debris, spectrum disputes.
  • Risk-appetite dependence — a risk-off macro (macro-regime) shuts the funding window.

  • Conviction 2.5/5 — a genuine long-run frontier with a large eventual TAM and real defense/
    connectivity demand, but the public vehicles are early, cash-burning, binary, and momentum-priced,

  • Invalidation: a string of launch/program failures + a closed funding window, OR SpaceX so
    dominating launch/broadband that public peers can't reach scale economics.
