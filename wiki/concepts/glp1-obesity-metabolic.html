GLP-1 / Obesity / Metabolic

Definition: GLP-1 receptor agonists and multi-agonist successors — incretin drugs that began as
type-2-diabetes therapies and are now the dominant obesity treatment. The theme spans branded
injectables (tirzepatide, semaglutide), the new oral class, next-gen multi-agonists, CDMO
capacity, and the second-order disruption to food, medtech and adjacent industries. The single most
powerful structural demand story in healthcare. Conviction 4 on the trend-radar.

Research/insight only — no buy/sell advice. Figures tagged (fact)/(est.)/(inference) with
period + source. "n/f" = no reliable figure found.

1. Definition & scope

Core: branded GLP-1 / GIP-GLP-1 drugs (Lilly tirzepatide — Mounjaro/Zepbound; Novo semaglutide —
Ozempic/Wegovy) plus the oral inflection (Lilly orforglipron/Foundayo; oral Wegovy) and next-gen
multi-agonists (retatrutide, CagriSema, amycretin, Amgen MariTide). Adjacencies: peptide/small-molecule
CDMOs (capacity was the chokepoint), telehealth distribution (Hims), and read-through to
medtech-devices (CGM tailwind; bariatric/ortho debate) and packaged food.

3. Key structural drivers

Demand: ~40%+ US adult obesity prevalence + rising globally; label expansion (cardiovascular,
sleep apnea, MASH, kidney, addiction) widens the eligible pool far beyond cosmetic weight loss; oral
formulations (orforglipron approved 2026; oral Wegovy ~1.3M scripts Q1) remove the injection barrier;
employer/payer + international reimbursement (Mounjaro on China NRDL). Supply: capacity was the
binding constraint — tens of $B of capex eased 2025 shortages; orals shift the cost curve (cheap
small molecules scale far better than peptides). Competition: duopoly opening to a broader field
(Viking, Amgen, Roche/Zealand) chasing ~20%+ efficacy.

4. Value-chain / linkage

Peptide/small-molecule API + CDMOs (historic chokepoint) → branded pharma (Lilly, Novo capture the
bulk of economics) → PBMs/payers gating access → widening prescriber base (now primary care via pills).
Second-order: tailwind for CGM/metabolic monitoring (medtech-devices); debated headwind for
bariatric surgery and some cardiac/ortho volumes; capital-allocation spillover into biotech-genomics
(Lilly/Novo war chests buying innovation); negative read for snacking/packaged food.

5. Competitive landscape

A two-firm oligopoly (Lilly + Novo) controls the overwhelming majority of revenue, with Lilly
pulling decisively ahead on efficacy (tirzepatide head-to-head) and the first unrestricted oral
(orforglipron). Moats: clinical efficacy/label breadth, manufacturing scale, the oral transition, and
pipeline depth (triple-agonist retatrutide in Ph3). Challengers (Viking VK2735, Amgen MariTide) compete
on mechanism/dosing convenience but are years behind commercialization. Viking is the most-cited M&A
target.

7. Competitiveness read

Lilly is clearly winning: superior head-to-head tirzepatide efficacy, the first unrestricted oral
(Foundayo/orforglipron), a triple-agonist (retatrutide) in Ph3, highest share (~60%), and Q1'26 +55%
growth with a guidance raise. Novo is the share-loser of the two — Ozempic fell ~8%, FY guidance
implies a sales contraction, and it leans on the oral Wegovy launch (~$354M beat) to stabilize; the
stock recovered on the pill beat even as franchise revenue contracts, so the narrative hinges on
CagriSema (US decision Q4'26). Among challengers, Viking has the cleanest emerging asset and is the
top M&A target; Amgen's MariTide showed strong absolute weight loss but disappointed some on
tolerability. Net: a one-horse race at the top with a deep but lagging chase pack.

8. Risks / what kills the thesis

  • Pricing/reimbursement compression — Medicare negotiation (IRA), payer pushback, and cheap orals
    could collapse net pricing faster than volume grows.
  • Competitive erosion — next-gen orals/multi-agonists fragment the duopoly post-2027.
  • Tolerability / long-term safety signals (GI, muscle loss) at population scale.
  • Patent cliffs — semaglutide composition-of-matter expiries open biosimilars later this decade.
  • Compounded/grey-market substitution pressuring branded volume.
  • Contested TAM — Goldman's cut shows demand-curve uncertainty; persistence unproven.

10. Our view — conviction / stage / horizon

  • Conviction 4/5 — durable, demographically-backed demand with expanding labels and the oral
    inflection just beginning; a genuine megatrend, not a fad. Held below 5 by contested TAM, pricing
    risk, and single-name dependence on Lilly.
  • Stage: accelerating. Horizon: 3–7 years.
  • Invalidation: evidence real-world persistence collapses, OR a payer-driven net-price reset that
    breaks the volume-for-price trade.
